Portal Final

Last Updated on Feb 27, 2020    ·    5 min read    ·    Hannah

I can't think when I'm alone. I really don't know why but to get things going upstairs there needs to be someone or something I can think with.I have always been like this; if, for example, a human isn't available and my dog is too busy licking his parts to be of any use, I split into two and argue with myself. Doing so in the streets forces my neighbors to change lanes in fear I might attempt to wear my shoes on my ears or something. Mental instability - in the eyes of the neighbors - is a price I usually pay for thinking. This means I have limited access to my thoughts. I get only a glimpse while the rest has to be squeezed out of me. A particularly interesting squeeze came from a conversation with my best friend from college.
"Everything here feels wrong, girl; nothing is done the right way," she said predicting my question.
"You mean your way? You have always been like this Hannah. You expect the world around you to follow your template of reality: correcting and controlling everything around you," I said motioning her to sit in fear she might physically fall apart any minute.
"I guess you're right," she said desperately trying to keep her red-shot eyes from closing. "I do go around hammering in anything that is out of place."
Right about then I felt I should say something wise. "I am sure you would be a whole lot better off if you just let the world be." I copied Gil Frondsal - my Zen teacher.
"I can't. I simply can't." She said in a hopeless manner. I wanted to help but didn't know how to. How do you tell someone that has a deep predilection for ordering the world around him to embrace chaos? I decided to give it a try and worry about the details later.
"But you know you would never succeed, right?" I said hoping she would tell me that she already knows. But she didn't. Instead she asked the question I still didn't have an answer to. "Why won't I?" She asked me defiantly.



I couldn't think of any reason why she wouldn't. It all seemed like nothing more than a failed attempt at psychotherapy. But then I said something that surprised both me and her.
"Physics"
Though I didn't know where I was going with it, I decided to hear myself out â€“ to be my own audience. "How?" she replied unsure.
"The universe tends to go from order to disorder following the path that requires the least amount of energy. Which means chaos is a fundamental property of the universe. It is possibly the only unbreakable law in Physics. So any attempts you make to order the world around you will fail - no matter your efforts."



It was only later that I realized I wasn't responsible for the Physicist-life coach lecture I did earlier. The conversation itself drew the ideas out from the dusty cauldrons they are stored in and string them together to make sense as a whole. I had no idea I was thinking it until it came out of me. I was, in effect, another audience to my own thoughts. It must've been my subconscious' doing in an effort to come up with a valid solution to Hannah's problem. Conversations like this are the maps of my own thinking spread across space and time, and the people that initiate them - Hannah, teachers, parents, and my imaginary friend that goes split when I'm alone - portals into my own mind.
